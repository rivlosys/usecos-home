How Ontario Income Tax is Calculated

Ontario uses a progressive tax system, meaning as you earn more, you pay a higher percentage on the incremental income. Your total tax bill is a combination of Federal Income Tax and Ontario Provincial Tax. Employers also deduct CPP (5.95%) and EI (1.66%) until you hit the yearly maximums.

Common Deductions in Ontario

When you look at your pay stub, you'll see several deductions. The CPP (Canada Pension Plan) is a mandatory contribution toward your retirement. In 2025, the maximum employee contribution is $4,075.75. EI (Employment Insurance) provides temporary financial assistance to unemployed Canadians. The maximum EI deduction for 2025 is $1,049.12.

Ontario Health Premium

Unlike some other provinces, Ontario also includes a Health Premium as part of its tax system for individuals earning over $20,000. This is usually bundled into your provincial tax withholding but is technically a separate fee based on income level.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is $100k a good salary in Ontario?

A $100,000 salary results in a take-home pay of approximately $6,130 per month. While well above the median, the "value" of this salary depends heavily on your housing costs, especially in high-demand areas like the GTA (Greater Toronto Area).

Why is my Ontario tax higher than other provinces?

Ontario's tax rates are generally lower than Quebec's or the Atlantic provinces but can be higher than Alberta or BC for middle-to-high income earners depending on specific tax brackets.

How many paychecks in a year in Ontario?

Most Ontario employees are paid bi-weekly, resulting in 26 pay periods per year. Some employers use a semi-monthly schedule (24 pay periods) or monthly (12 pay periods).
