How California Income Tax Works

Your California paycheck is subject to four main deductions before it reaches your bank account: Federal Income Tax, Social Security (6.2%), Medicare (1.45%), and California State Disability Insurance (SDI), alongside the California State Income Tax itself. California's tax brackets are among the most progressive in the nation, with rates climbing quickly for middle and high earners.

Understanding FICA Deductions

FICA stands for the Federal Insurance Contributions Act. It consists of two parts: Social Security and Medicare. For 2025, you pay 6.2% of your gross wages into Social Security up to a specific wage base cap ($176,100 projected). Medicare is 1.45% on all earnings, with an additional 0.9% tax for earners making over $200,000.

California SDI and Voluntary Plans

Most California employees also see a deduction for State Disability Insurance (SDI). While this rate fluctuates, it typically hovers around 1% of your wages, providing coverage for non-work-related illnesses, injuries, or paid family leave.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is $100k a good salary in California?

While $100,000 is significantly higher than the national average, in California metros like the Bay Area or Orange County, it is often considered a "middle-class" income due to extremely high rent and cost of living.

How can I lower my California tax bill?

Contributing to a 401(k) or a Traditional IRA can reduce your taxable income at both the federal and state levels. California also offers various credits for renters and families with children.

Does California tax Social Security benefits?

No, California is one of the states that does not tax Social Security retirement benefits, which is a major plus for retirees living in the state.
