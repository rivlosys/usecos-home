How Much Income Do You Actually Need for a $700,000 Home?

In the current Canadian real estate landscape, the $700,000 price point represents a significant milestone for many families. However, qualifying for a mortgage of this size involves more than just a decent credit score. Lenders look at a combination of your Gross Household Income, your Existing Debt, and your Down Payment.

The Mathematical Reality of Qualifying in 2026

Lenders use two primary debt-service ratios to determine if you can afford a $700k home:

  • Gross Debt Service (GDS): This covers your housing costs (Mortgage, Property Tax, Heating, and 50% of Condo fees). This should not exceed 39% of your pre-tax income.
  • Total Debt Service (TDS): This includes all the costs of GDS plus any other debt obligations like car loans, student loans, or credit card balances. This should not exceed 44% of your income.

Provincial Variations: Ontario vs. BC vs. Alberta

While the federal mortgage rules are the same, your Land Transfer Tax and Property Tax vary by province, which affects your "cash to close" and monthly affordability:

  • Ontario: Buying a $700k home requires approximately $10,475 in Land Transfer Tax. If you are in Toronto, this doubles to roughly $20,950.
  • British Columbia: You will face a Property Transfer Tax of roughly $12,000.
  • Alberta: With no Land Transfer Tax, Alberta remains the most affordable for closing costs, requiring only a few hundred dollars for registration fees.

Three Ways to Qualify with a Lower Income

  1. Reduce Consumer Debt: A $500 monthly car payment can reduce your mortgage borrowing power by as much as $75,000. Paying off high-interest debt is the fastest way to increase your TDS room.
  2. The 30-Year Amortization: If you put 20% down, you can extend your amortization to 30 years. This lowers the monthly payment, helping you meet GDS/TDS limits on a smaller salary.
  3. Add a Co-Signer: Adding the income of a spouse or family member can bridge the gap, provided their own debt ratios are clean.
